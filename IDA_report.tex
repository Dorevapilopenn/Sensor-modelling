\reportbox{Report Summary}{
There are situations where a species needs to be classified into a class; in order
for that, information about the species must be gathered by a sensor. This report
discusses IDAs and mixed-host sensor arrays and the conditions in which these
yield maximum efficiency of classification. The simulations compare single
component IDA, two-element IDA arrays, mixed-host arrays, and mixed-host sensors
using PLS-DA classification of simulated UV--vis signal vectors.
}

\section{Background}

Chemosensors are sensors which are governed by rules of chemical equilibrium,
and the signal varies by the conditions of the equilibrium, i.e. the initial
concentrations and equilibrium constants.

Indicator Displacement Assays (IDAs) produce the signal by variations in
concentrations of signal-producing species, which then is measured. The goal of
this paper is to assess whether the additional equilibrium in mixed-host sensors
affects the efficiency of classification.

\section{Sensor Setup}

The sensors are used in a sensor of two sensor elements, which we will refer to
as a sensor array. The sensor components in both sensors are Host (H) and Dye
(D), with equal initial concentrations $C_s$, and the Guest (G) is the analyte
with the initial concentration of $C_0$.

\subsection{IDA Array}

There are six formation constants in the IDA array. For each sensor element, the
host binds the dye and the two guests:
\begin{align}
\mathrm{H} + \mathrm{D} &\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{HD} \qquad \KHD,\\
\mathrm{H} + \mathrm{G}_1 &\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{HG}_1 \qquad \KHG{}_1,\\
\mathrm{H} + \mathrm{G}_2 &\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{HG}_2 \qquad \KHG{}_2.
\end{align}

\subsection{Mixed-Host Sensor}

Adding an additional reaction to the IDA results in what we will refer to as
mixed-host sensors. The additional equilibrium is a guest--dye reaction:
\begin{align}
\mathrm{D} + \mathrm{G}_1 &\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{DG}_1 \qquad \KDG{}_1,\\
\mathrm{D} + \mathrm{G}_2 &\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{DG}_2 \qquad \KDG{}_2.
\end{align}
We are interested in knowing the effect of this added reaction and studying them.

\section{Classification Rules}

For a better intuitive understanding, we define two classes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Class 1}: the species with a smaller formation constant in the
    first sensor element of the array.
    \item \textbf{Class 2}: the species with a greater formation constant in the
    first sensor element of the array.
\end{itemize}

\section{Simulation Method}

The six formation constants are chosen from a pool $P$:
\[
K_i \in P \quad \mathrm{for\ every}\ i,\qquad
P=\{1,2.5,4,5.5,7,8.5,10,11.5,13\}.
\]

For each permutation, a random set of $C_0$ values (90 concentrations for each
class), keeping $C_s$ constant, is used to simulate the UV--vis signal of D and
HD species for each set, so an according set of signal vectors is yielded. After
splitting into a training and test set, a PLS-DA model is deployed to conduct the
classification.

There are two settings for the sensor arrays:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{N}$: Class 2 has a greater $\KHG$ in S1 but a smaller $\KHG$
    in S2.
    \item $\mathbf{P}$: Class 2 has a higher $\KHG$ both in S1 and S2.
    \item $\mathbf{U}$: for ease of notation, the union of P and N.
\end{itemize}

\begin{enumerate}

    \item A low $\Delta D$ so the formation constant for the guest with greater
    affinity is almost as great as $\KHD$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Report Takeaways}

The two-element IDA array improves classification relative to the single
component IDA by increasing $\Et$ and lowering the variability in differentiation
efficiency. For IDA arrays, the practical design rule is to keep both
$\log\KHD$ values around 8, maintain $\Delta HD$ close to zero, increase
$\Delta K$, and tune $\Delta D$ so that the stronger guest approaches the
host--dye affinity. For the Ma mixed-host array, the generated data show high
mean efficiency across the union partition and maxima at $\Et=1$, suggesting
that the second mixed-host element helps maintain robust differentiation across
a broad parameter region. For the Ms single component mixed-host sensor, the
added dye--guest reaction changes the important design knobs: $\Delta G$,
$\Delta C$, and $\Delta D$ become the dominant variables, with best
differentiation occurring when dye--guest contrast is at the low end of the
sampled $\Delta G$ range, $\Delta C$ is positive, and $\Delta D$ remains near
$-1.5$.
